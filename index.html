Learn about the dataset selection

The rationale behind choosing this dataset lies in its inclusion of a diverse array of columns, capturing essential facets of biodiversity occurrences. It encompasses identifiers like scientific name and occurrence, facilitating precise data referencing. Taxonomic details, including kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species, offer insights into the biological classification of the recorded organisms.

Geographic information such as latitude and longitude provides a spatial context, crucial for understanding the distribution of species. Temporal aspects, represented by eventdate, day, month, and year, contribute to the dataset's temporal dimension, allowing for the analysis of seasonal patterns and trends.

Furthermore, attributes like country, locality, province, and recordedby add contextual information, enhancing the dataset's richness. spatiotemporal dynamics.

OUR WORK

Statement of the hypothesis

The hypothesis proposes that the migration patterns of the species Passer domesticus are influenced by the season of the year, particularly the month, and that the choice of destination countries is determined by specific characteristics. The suggestion is that, during winter, Passer domesticus is likely to migrate to countries situated closer to the equator. This implies a correlation between the seasonal changes, geographical location, and the migratory behavior of Passer domesticus.

MACHINE LEARNING

Selection I

  • Your objectives for estimation using the provided data
    • Our primary goal is to build a predictive model capable of estimating the geographic location (country) of the Passer domesticus species based on climatic and temporal variables. This endeavor aims to deepen our understanding of how environmental factors, such as temperature, precipitation, and seasonality, influence the migratory patterns and spatial distribution of this bird species. Additionally, the model could facilitate predictions regarding the potential presence of the species in different regions, which would prove invaluable for ecological and conservation studies.
  • Details about your initial model selection
    • The initial choice of the multinomial logistic regression model was driven by the nature of the target variable, which is the numerical code representing the country. Given the presence of multiple possible classes (countries), multinomial logistic regression is well-suited to address this multiclass classification problem effectively.
  • Information on validation methods and the metrics employed
    1. Precision: Precision measures the accuracy of positive predictions for each class. For example, class 1 has a precision of 0.63, indicating that 63% of instances predicted as class 1 were correct. However, precision is low or zero for many other classes, suggesting challenges in accurately predicting these classes.
    2. Recall: Recall reflects the model's ability to correctly identify all instances of a class. A recall of 0.91 for class 1 means that 91% of actual class 1 instances were predicted correctly. Other classes have low or zero recall values, indicating that many instances of these classes were missed by the model.
    3. F1-score: The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balanced measure of a model's performance. Higher F1-scores indicate better balance between precision and recall. Some classes have reasonable F1-scores (e.g., class 1 with an F1-score of 0.74), while others have F1-scores of 0 due to zero precision or recall.
  • Preliminary conclusions drawn from your analysis to date.
    1. There are moderate correlations between some climatic variables and geographic location (country), suggesting that these factors may influence the migratory patterns of the Passer domesticus species.
    2. The current multinomial logistic regression model has relatively low accuracy (around 60%), indicating that there is still room for improvement in its predictive capacity.
    3. The dataset shows that certain countries have a greater number of observations of the Passer domesticus bird, which could be related to specific environmental or geographical factors in those regions. It is important to consider these patterns and potential biases in the data when interpreting the model results.
